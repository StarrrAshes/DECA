About DECA

Our History

From Distributive Education Clubs to a Global Organization

DECA Inc., formerly known as the Distributive Education Clubs of America, was established in 1946 when the United States Office of Education invited state supervisors of distributive education to Washington, D.C. to develop plans for a national student organization. The first Interstate Conference of Distributive Education Clubs was held in Memphis, Tennessee, in April 1947, where delegates from 17 charter member states adopted a constitution and officially named the organization.

The national headquarters were built in Reston, Virginia, in 1976. In 1991, the Board of Directors decided the acronym "DECA" would stand on its own, moving away from its original expanded name to reflect the organization's broadened scope beyond distributive education.

Key Milestones

DECA Through the Decades

1946 — Organization Founded

The U.S. Office of Education convened a committee to form a national organization of Distributive Education Clubs. DECA was officially launched.

1947 — First National Conference

The first Interstate Conference was held in Memphis, Tennessee, with delegates from 12 states voting to form the national organization.

1948 — Constitution Adopted

At the second national conference in St. Louis, Missouri, 17 charter member states adopted the constitution and officially named the group "Distributive Education Clubs of America."

1976 — National Headquarters Built

The National DECA Center was dedicated in Reston, Virginia, providing a permanent home for the organization's development and coordination activities.

1988 — U.S. Department of Education Endorsement

Secretary of Education William Bennett endorsed DECA and other vocational student organizations, affirming their role in career and technical education.

1991 — Rebranding to "DECA"

The Board of Directors decided the acronym "DECA" would stand alone, paired with the tagline "An Association of Marketing Students," reflecting its broader mission.

Today — A Global Community

DECA has grown to more than 300,000 members in all 50 U.S. states, Washington D.C., and multiple countries including Canada, Germany, China, Spain, and more. The organization continues to evolve its programs and competitive events to prepare students for the modern workforce.

Organization Structure

Two Divisions, One Mission

DECA is organized into two student divisions, each with programs tailored to its members' learning styles, interests, and academic focus:

High School Division: With over 292,000 members in more than 4,000 chapters, the high school division is the largest. Students in grades 9–12 join their school's DECA chapter, participate in competitions, and develop leadership skills under the guidance of a faculty advisor.

Collegiate Division: Formerly known as Delta Epsilon Chi (until July 1, 2010), the collegiate division includes over 5,400 members in 200+ colleges and universities. Collegiate members compete in 25 different events at the International Career Development Conference.

DECA is governed by an Executive Director (Frank Peterson), an eleven-member Board of Directors, a National Advisory Board of business partners, and a bipartisan Congressional Advisory Board of U.S. Senators and Representatives.

Guiding Principles

DECA's Core Values

DECA's four core values are represented in the four inner points of the DECA Diamond, the organization's official logo.

Value

Competence

DECA members demonstrate academic knowledge and technical skills. Research shows that the more engaged members are with DECA, the higher their GPAs and the more likely they are to pursue challenging coursework.

Value

Innovation

DECA encourages creative problem-solving and entrepreneurial thinking. Members learn to identify opportunities, develop business plans, and create innovative solutions to real-world challenges.

Value

Integrity

Ethical decision-making is central to DECA's programs. Members learn professional responsibility and community-oriented leadership, preparing them to be responsible citizens and business leaders.

Value

Teamwork

DECA transforms individuals into team players who understand that their success is linked to the success of others. Team Decision Making events and chapter activities reinforce collaborative skills.
